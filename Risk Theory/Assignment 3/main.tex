For a certain branch of an insurance company, weekly \textit{loss ratios} were recorded, which are the total claims received each week divided by the total premium earned that week, as a rounded percentage. The total recording period was 100 weeks. Of course big loss ratios are a reason for concern for the risk management department. Based on these data, they want to estimate the probabilities of ``large'' and ``extreme'' loss ratios, and consider taking a stop-loss like reinsurance to cover the extreme loss ratios.

% ============================================================
\section{Estimating the cumulants}
% ============================================================
The 100 (rounded) observed loss ratios are stored, not in the order that they occurred but in increasing order, into a vector \texttt{X} like this:

\begin{lstlisting}
X <- scan(text="
 68  68  68  69  69  69  69  69  70  70  70  71  71  72  72  72  73  73  73
 73  74  74  74  74  74  75  75  76  76  76  77  78  78  79  80  80  81  81
 81  81  82  82  82  82  83  83  83  84  84  85  85  87  87  87  89  90  91
 91  91  92  92  92  92  92  93  93  94  95  97  97  97  97  98  98  98  98
 99 100 101 101 102 102 103 103 104 106 107 108 112 113 116 116 117 126 127
132 132 149 158 170")
\end{lstlisting}

We assume that these numbers are i.i.d.\ observations from some random variable $X$ of unknown distribution. To be able to use the various approximations for this distribution, we first have to estimate the first three cumulants of the random variable $X$. These cumulants, as we have seen in the lectures, are the mean $\kappa_1 = \mu = \mathrm{E}[X]$, the variance $\kappa_2 = \sigma^2 = \mathrm{E}[(X-\mu)^2]$ and the third central moment $\kappa_3 = \mathrm{E}[(X-\mu)^3]$. We estimate these quantities from the data as follows:

\begin{lstlisting}
k1 <- mean(X); k2 <- mean((X-k1)^2); k3 <- mean((X-k1)^3)
k1; k2; k3 ## 90  381.2  11841.6
\end{lstlisting}

With a sample size of $n = 100$ it does not make a lot of difference if we choose denominator $n$ for the sample variance, as we have done above, or $n-1$, as is done in the R-function \texttt{var}. The small sample correction of $n-1$ instead of $n$ is applied to obtain unbiased estimates. But using unbiased estimates for $\kappa_2$ and $\kappa_3$ does not necessarily mean that quantities like $\gamma$ (skewness of $X$) or $\Pr[X > x]$ or $\mathrm{E}[(X-d)_+]$ are estimated without bias. In any case, the simple method-of-moments estimators we use are consistent.

\section{Large loss ratios, normal approximation}
% ============================================================
Suppose that any loss ratio larger than 160\% is considered to be a large loss ratio by the insurer.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[$\boldsymbol{Q_2}$]
    \begin{enumerate}
      \item[(a)] Use the normal approximation to estimate the probability $p$ that the loss ratio $X$ on any given week is 160\% or less. Use a correction for continuity, since the random variable $X$ is integer-valued but we approximate it with a continuous (normal) distribution. (That is, approximate the probability $\Pr[X \leq 160.5]$ rather than $\Pr[X \leq 160]$.)\\ A:

\begin{lstlisting}
p_norm <- pnorm(160.5, mean=k1, sd=sqrt(k2))
p_norm  ## 0.9998474
\end{lstlisting}

      The normal approximation gives
      \[
        p = \Pr[X \leq 160.5] \approx \Phi\!\left(\frac{160.5 - 90}{\sqrt{381.2}}\right) = \Phi(3.611) \approx 0.99985.
      \]

      \item[(b)] Assuming that $p$ in part (a) is the real probability of observing a loss ratio of 160\% or less, compute the probability that the maximum loss ratio in a sample of 100 loss ratios is 160\% or less. Is this a likely occurrence according to the normal approximation? What does this tell you about the quality of the normal approximation in this case?\\ A:

\begin{lstlisting}
p_norm^100  ## 0.9849
\end{lstlisting}

      Under the normal approximation, the probability that \textit{all} 100 observations are at most 160\% is
      \[
        p^{100} \approx 0.99985^{100} \approx 0.9849.
      \]
      This means there is only a $1 - 0.9849 = 1.51\%$ chance of observing any value above 160\% in a sample of 100. Since our sample does contain the value 170, this is a very unlikely event under the normal approximation. This tells us the normal approximation underestimates the probability of large loss ratios, and is a poor fit for the right tail of the distribution.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

\section{Large loss ratios, NP approximation}
% ============================================================
Judging from the normal approximation, a sample value of more than 160\% would be too rare to have reasonably occurred in our sample. Also, the histogram does not look like a normal density, exhibiting a highly asymmetric shape. Most likely, the normal power (NP) approximation will give us better results, since it takes the skewness of the underlying distribution into account.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item[$\boldsymbol{Q_3}$]
    \begin{enumerate}
      \item[(a)] Estimate the skewness $\gamma = \kappa_3/\kappa_2^{1.5}$ of $X$ from the sample $X$, store the skewness value as \texttt{gam}. \\ A:

\begin{lstlisting}
gam <- k3 / k2^1.5
gam  ## 1.591040
\end{lstlisting}

      The estimated skewness is $\hat{\gamma} = 11841.6 \,/\, 381.2^{1.5} \approx 1.591$. The positive value confirms the right-skewed shape visible in the histogram.

      \item[(b)] Use the NP approximation, MART (2.60), to estimate the probability $p$ that the loss ratio $X$ on any given week is 160\% or less. Use a correction for continuity as in Q2. \\ A:

      The NP equation to solve for $w$ given standardised value $z$ is
      \[
        z = w + \frac{\gamma}{6}(w^2 - 1),
      \]
      which is a quadratic in $w$: $\frac{\gamma}{6}w^2 + w - \bigl(z + \frac{\gamma}{6}\bigr) = 0$, giving the closed-form solution
      \[
        w = \frac{-1 + \sqrt{1 + 4\,\frac{\gamma}{6}\bigl(z + \frac{\gamma}{6}\bigr)}}{2\,\frac{\gamma}{6}}.
      \]

\begin{lstlisting}
np_w <- function(x) {
  z  <- (x - k1) / sqrt(k2)
  a  <- gam / 6;  b <- 1;  cc <- -(z + gam/6)
  (-b + sqrt(b^2 - 4*a*cc)) / (2*a)
}
w_160 <- np_w(160.5)
p_np  <- pnorm(w_160)
p_np  ## 0.9912812
\end{lstlisting}

      The NP approximation gives $w = 2.377$ and
      \[
        p = \Phi(2.377) \approx 0.9913.
      \]
      This is smaller than the normal approximation value of 0.99985, which is the heavier right tail implied by the positive skewness.
\newpage
      \item[(c)] Assuming that $p$ in part (b) is the real probability of observing a loss ratio of 160\% or less, compute the probability that the maximum loss ratio in a sample of 100 loss ratios is 160\% or less. Is it now likely to observe a maximum observation exceeding 160\% in a sample of 100 loss ratios? \\ A:

\begin{lstlisting}
p_np^100  ## 0.4166
\end{lstlisting}

      Under the NP approximation,
      \[
        p^{100} \approx 0.9913^{100} \approx 0.417.
      \]
      The probability that at least one observation in 100 exceeds 160\% is therefore $1 - 0.417 = 58.3\%$. This means it is actually more likely than not to observe a maximum above 160\% in a sample of 100, which is consistent with what we observe in the data. The NP approximation is a much more plausible model for the tail than the normal approximation.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

\section{Extreme loss ratios, translated gamma approximation}
% ============================================================

Suppose that any loss ratio larger than 170\% is considered to be an extreme loss ratio by the insurer. During the 100 weeks in our observation period, a loss ratio of over 170\% did not occur, but the probability of this event will no doubt be greater than zero. To give a better estimate than the normal approximation, we assume that the (unrounded) loss ratio $X$ has a translated gamma distribution, that is, $X \sim U + x_0$ with $U \sim \mathrm{gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$. To compute the parameters $x_0$, $\alpha$ and $\beta$, we will again use the sample cumulants \texttt{k1}, \texttt{k2} and \texttt{k3} as estimates for $\mu = \mathrm{E}[X]$, $\sigma^2 = \mathrm{Var}[X]$, and $\kappa_3 = \mathrm{E}[(X-\mu)^3]$.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item[$\boldsymbol{Q_4}$]
    \begin{enumerate}
      \item[] Fill in the dots in the script below to estimate the probability of a loss ratio exceeding 170\%. See (2.56) and (2.57) in MART. Use continuity correction as before.

      For the last line, you might want to consult \texttt{?dgamma}. Which of the four functions \texttt{dgamma}, \texttt{pgamma}, \texttt{qgamma} and \texttt{rgamma} do you need here?\\ A:

\begin{lstlisting}
mu    <- k1
sigma <- sqrt(k2)
gam   <- k3 / k2^1.5

alpha <- 4 / gam^2
beta  <- 2 / (sigma * gam)
x0    <- mu - alpha / beta

1 - pgamma(170.5 - x0, shape=alpha, rate=beta)  ## 0.004249
\end{lstlisting}

      Using MART (2.57), the translated gamma parameters are
      \[
        \alpha = \frac{4}{\gamma^2} \approx 1.580, \quad
        \beta  = \frac{2}{\sigma\gamma} \approx 0.06438, \quad
        x_0    = \mu - \frac{\alpha}{\beta} \approx 65.46.
      \]
      We need pgamma, since we want a cumulative probability. With the continuity correction, the estimated probability of a loss ratio over 170\% is
      \[
        \Pr[X > 170] \approx 1 - G(170.5 - x_0;\, \alpha, \beta) \approx 0.00425,
      \]
      where $G(\,\cdot\,;\alpha,\beta)$ is the $\mathrm{gamma}(\alpha,\beta)$ cdf.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

\section{A stop-loss insurance covering extreme loss ratios}
% ============================================================
To protect itself against extreme weekly losses, the insurer has found a reinsurer willing to pay $X - 170$ (in some monetary unit), if the loss ratio $X$ (in \%) exceeds 170. To find the net premium $\mathrm{E}[(X-170)_+]$ for this reinsurance, the corresponding sample quantity will not help, since a loss ratio above 170\% has not occurred up to now. Therefore we use a translated gamma approximation to find a net premium for this stop-loss reinsurance contract. As an aside, first we give some remarks on the use of a correction for continuity.

In the figure below, we approximate the discrete cdf $F \sim \mathrm{Uniform}\{1,2,3,4\}$ by the continuous cdf $G \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(0.5, 4.5)$, which has the same expected value (2.5) as $F$. For approximating $F(k)$ at integer $k$ it seems better to use $G(k+0.5)$ rather than $G(k)$, which justifies the use of a continuity correction. However, for approximating the stop-loss premium of $F$ at e.g.\ $d = 2$ (gray area), it is better to compute the stop-loss premium of $G$ (triangular area above the red line) at argument $d = 2$ rather than $d = 2.5$. So when approximating stop-loss premiums, it is best not to use the correction for continuity.

\subsection*{Net premium for an alternative reinsurance contract}
Now imagine an alternative reinsurance contract that pays an amount 1 (again in some monetary unit) in case the loss ratio $X$ (as an integer) is larger than 140 and less than or equal to 160, 2 if the loss ratio is larger than 160 and less than or equal to 180, and 3 if it is larger than 180. Using the indicator function notation (MART (2.33)), the payment $Y$ of the reinsurance company can be written as
\[
  Y = I_{(140,160]}(X) + 2\,I_{(160,180]}(X) + 3\,I_{(180,\infty)}(X).
\]
Therefore, the net premium for this reinsurance contract is
\begin{align*}
  \mathrm{E}[Y] &= \Pr[140 < X \leq 160] + 2\,\Pr[160 < X \leq 180] + 3\,\Pr[X > 180]\\
                &= \Pr[X > 140] + \Pr[X > 160] + \Pr[X > 180].
\end{align*}

\section{Censored observations}
% ============================================================
Suppose we find out that the 100 weekly loss ratios stored in $X$ actually come from an observation period of 170 weeks rather than 100 weeks. The 70 loss ratios not recorded in $X$ were all less than or equal to 67\%, and their exact values were not recorded because the insurance company is only interested in loss ratios exceeding 67\%. This means that we have a left-censored dataset: for some data points (the ones recorded in $X$) we know the exact value, but for the other data points (the 70 loss ratios not recorded in $X$) we only have partial information, namely that they are below a certain threshold value.

Censoring should be taken into account when we fit a distribution to the dataset. In this section, we will once again use a normal approximation for the dataset $X$, but when we estimate the parameters of that normal distribution, we will take into account the fact that there are 70 ``censored'' observations less than or equal to 67\%.

If there were no censored data at all, the likelihood function of the normal parameters $\mu, \sigma$ based on the observations $x_1, \ldots, x_{100}$ in $X$ would be:
\[
  L(\mu,\sigma;\,x_1,\ldots,x_{100}) = \prod_{i=1}^{100}\bigl[\Phi(x_i+0.5;\mu,\sigma) - \Phi(x_i-0.5;\mu,\sigma)\bigr],
\]
where $\Phi(\,\cdot\,;\mu,\sigma)$ denotes the normal cdf with parameters $\mu$ and $\sigma$. This expression follows from the fact that under the normal assumption, the rounded loss ratio $X$ will be equal to $x_i$ with probability $\Phi(x_i+0.5;\mu,\sigma) - \Phi(x_i-0.5;\mu,\sigma)$.

With 70 censored observations less than or equal to 67, the likelihood function becomes:
\[
  L(\mu,\sigma;\,x_1,\ldots,x_{100}) = \Phi(67.5;\mu,\sigma)^{70}\prod_{i=1}^{100}\bigl[\Phi(x_i+0.5;\mu,\sigma) - \Phi(x_i-0.5;\mu,\sigma)\bigr],
\]
so each censored observation contributes a factor of $\Phi(67.5;\mu,\sigma)$, the probability of being less than 67.5, to the likelihood. Therefore the log-likelihood with censoring is:
\begin{align*}
  \log L(\mu,\sigma;\,x_1,\ldots,x_{100})
  = 70\log\bigl[\Phi(67.5;\mu,\sigma)\bigr]
  + \sum_{i=1}^{100}\log\bigl[\Phi(x_i+0.5;\mu,\sigma) - \Phi(x_i-0.5;\mu,\sigma)\bigr].
\end{align*}
The maximum likelihood estimates for $\mu$ and $\sigma$ under censoring can be found by numerically maximizing this log-likelihood function.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item[$\boldsymbol{Q_9}$]
    \begin{enumerate}
      \item[(a)] Write an R function called \texttt{negloglik} that computes the negative log-likelihood function with censoring, using observations in $X$. The function should take one vector argument \texttt{mu.sig}, with \texttt{mu.sig[1]} corresponding to $\mu$ and \texttt{mu.sig[2]} corresponding to $\sigma$. \\A:
\begin{lstlisting}
negloglik <- function(mu.sig) {
  mu_  <- mu.sig[1]
  sig_ <- mu.sig[2]
  if (sig_ <= 0) return(Inf)
  cens_ll <- 70 * log(pnorm(67.5, mean=mu_, sd=sig_))
  obs_ll  <- sum(log(pnorm(X + 0.5, mean=mu_, sd=sig_) -
                     pnorm(X - 0.5, mean=mu_, sd=sig_)))
  -(cens_ll + obs_ll)
